SOC meets GRC
Most cybersecurity courses teach SOC and GRC as separate subjects. In the real world they are two sides of the same coin. This project shows how they work together — the SOC detects what is happening, GRC makes sure the right controls exist to prevent it.

SOC — Security Operations Center

THE REACTIVE SIDE
  • 🔍Monitor logs 24/7
  • 🚨Detect suspicious patterns
  • 🔬Investigate alerts
  • 🛡️Respond to incidents
  • 📊Track threat intelligence
  • 📋Document findings

GRC — Governance, Risk, Compliance

THE PROACTIVE SIDE
  • 📄Write security policies
  • ⚠️Identify and score risks
  • ✅Verify controls are working
  • 📏Audit against ISO 27001 / NIST
  • 🌐Scan network exposure
  • 📊Generate compliance reports
SOC
A team monitoring systems in real time, responding to alerts as they happen.

GRC explained
GRC is the strategic layer. It answers the questions the SOC cannot — are our controls right? Can we prove they work? What are our biggest risks right now?

The loop between SOC and GRC
This is the most important insight in the whole project. SOC and GRC are not separate functions. They feed each other in a continuous loop. Understanding this loop is what separates a good candidate from an average one in interviews.
One continuous security loop
GRC defines the rules. SOC enforces them. SOC findings improve GRC. Round and round.
1
🔍
SOC detects a threat
The alert engine fires on brute force SSH from a known malicious IP. AbuseIPDB confirms the source is a repeat offender.
SOC
2
📋
Finding goes into risk register
The repeated attack pattern is added to the GRC risk register. Likelihood score increases. The risk level goes from Medium to High.
GRC
3
📄
Policy gets updated
The security policy is updated to require SSH key authentication instead of passwords. A new patch management requirement is added for the affected systems.
GRC
4
⚙️
New detection rule added
The SOC adds a rule to detect any password-based SSH login attempts — now a policy violation. The threshold is tightened based on the attack pattern observed.
SOC
5
📊
Compliance score improves
The weekly GRC report shows the Access Control compliance score has improved. The risk is downgraded. The post-incident report is filed and the loop begins again.
BOTH
Why this matters in an interview: If you can describe this loop confidently — with a concrete example — you demonstrate that you understand security as a system, not just a collection of tools. Most candidates cannot do this. It is the answer that gets you the job.

SOC and GRC together cover the full spectrum of cybersecurity operations
0 / 12 topics covered
  • What a SOC does and why it exists
    Monitor, detect, investigate, respond. The four pillars of security operations.
  • How log parsing works
    Logs are the raw material. Parsing turns unstructured text into structured data that rules can match against.
  • MITRE ATT&CK tactics and techniques
    Tactics are goals. Techniques are methods. T1110 is brute force under Credential Access.
  • What a detection rule does
    A rule defines what pattern to look for, what threshold to apply and what action to take when it fires.
  • What threat intelligence adds to an alert
    Context. A 97% AbuseIPDB score changes a suspicious login into a targeted attack from a known threat actor.
  • What GRC stands for and what each part means
    Governance is rules. Risk is prioritisation. Compliance is proving controls work in an audit.
  • How risk scoring works
    Likelihood x impact. Scores 1-4 Low, 5-9 Medium, 10-16 High, 17-25 Critical.
  • What ISO 27001 is
    The international information security management standard. 93 controls across four themes. External audit for certification.
  • What NIST CSF covers
    Six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. A complete security lifecycle.
  • Why network scanning matters for GRC
    Policies say what should be closed. The scanner verifies what actually is. That gap is the risk.
  • How SOC findings feed back into GRC
    Incidents raise risk scores. Policy gets updated. SOC gets new rules. The loop continues.
  • Why the SOC/GRC loop matters in an interview
    Because most candidates treat them as separate. Explaining the loop shows strategic security thinking.
