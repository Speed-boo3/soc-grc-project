Detection Engineering
MITRE ATT&CK coverage

Every detection rule in this project maps to a MITRE ATT&CK technique. This is the industry standard for categorising attacker behaviour and is referenced in every enterprise security tool and job description.

💡
Why MITRE ATT&CK matters
When an alert fires, the technique ID tells you exactly what the attacker is trying to accomplish and what they are likely to do next. This enables faster triage and more targeted response — which is why every enterprise SIEM, EDR and XDR platform uses ATT&CK as its detection taxonomy.

Integration
The feedback loop

SOC and GRC are not parallel tracks. They form a continuous loop. Every detection feeds governance. Every policy creates a new rule. This is what mature security operations looks like.

Continuous improvement
Security is a process, not a state
The loop never ends. Each cycle makes detection faster and governance stronger.
1
SOC
Alert fires
Brute force SSH from 45.33.32.156. AbuseIPDB returns 97% abuse confidence. Classified as a targeted attack, not noise.
2
GRC
Risk register updated
The attack pattern raises the likelihood score on RISK-001 (Unpatched Systems / SSH Exposure) from 3 to 5. Level goes Critical.
3
GRC
Policy updated
Security policy amended: SSH key authentication now required, password authentication disabled. Patch deadline shortened to 7 days.
4
SOC
New detection rule
Alert rule added for any password-based SSH attempt — now a policy violation. Threshold tightened from 5 to 3 attempts.
5
BOTH
Compliance improves
Next week's GRC report shows Access Control score up 12%. Risk downgraded. Post-incident report filed. The loop starts again.
🎯
Why this matters in an interview at a large organisation
Enterprise security teams are not looking for people who know one tool. They want people who understand how detection, risk management and compliance connect. Describing this loop — with a concrete example — demonstrates system-level security thinking. Most candidates cannot do it.
Incident Response
NIST SP 800-61 lifecycle

Every alert that escalates to an incident follows this structure. The playbook exists so analysts do not have to make decisions under pressure.

1
Triage
Assess and classify
Is this a real incident or a false positive? Check source IP, time, user agent, context. Do not act until you know what you are dealing with.
Target: 5 minutes
2
Containment
Stop the spread
Block the source IP. Isolate the affected system if necessary. The priority is preventing lateral movement.
HIGH alerts: 30 minutes
3
Investigation
Build the timeline
What did the attacker do? Did they succeed? Which systems were touched? Construct a complete evidence-based timeline.
Complexity-dependent
4
Eradication and Recovery
Remove and restore
Remove the threat. Patch the vulnerability. Verify clean state. Restore services with continuous monitoring in place.
Before full restoration
5
Post-Incident
Learn and improve
Write the report. What worked? What failed? Update the detection rules. Improve the playbook. Feed findings into the risk register.
Within 5 business days

These are the topics that come up in SOC and GRC interviews at large organisations. Tick what you understand. Each item maps to something this project covers in practice.

Career readiness
Enterprise security roles demand both technical and strategic fluency
0 / 12 topics assessed
  • Explain what a SOC does and how it is structured
    Tiers, escalation paths, the difference between L1 triage and L2 investigation.
  • Describe the log parsing pipeline end to end
    Raw file to structured JSON to rule matching to alert. The data flow an engineer would implement.
  • Map a log entry to a MITRE ATT&CK technique
    Given a raw log, identify the tactic and technique. Explain what the attacker is trying to accomplish.
  • Explain why a sliding window is better than a total count for brute force detection
    Attack rate matters more than total. A burst of 10 in 30 seconds is more significant than 10 spread over a week.
  • Use threat intelligence to contextualise an alert
    An IP with 97% AbuseIPDB confidence is not a misconfigured client. It changes the severity and response.
  • Define GRC and explain each component without jargon
    Governance is rules and accountability. Risk is prioritisation. Compliance is proof that controls work.
  • Score a risk using likelihood and impact, and explain the treatment decision
    Not just calculating the number — explaining why a score of 20 means immediate action and who owns it.
  • Describe ISO 27001 and what certification requires
    93 controls, four themes, Statement of Applicability, external audit, surveillance reviews.
  • Explain the six NIST CSF functions and how they relate
    Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover — and why the order is deliberate.
  • Explain why network scanning is a GRC activity
    Policy says what should be closed. The scanner verifies what actually is. The gap is a documented risk.
  • Describe the SOC-GRC feedback loop with a concrete example
    An alert raises a risk score. A policy is updated. A new detection rule is created. The loop continues.
  • Walk through the NIST SP 800-61 incident response lifecycle
    Preparation, Detection, Containment, Eradication, Recovery, Post-Incident — with timing expectations for each phase.
